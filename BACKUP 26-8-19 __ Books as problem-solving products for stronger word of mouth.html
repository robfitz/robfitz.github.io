SUMMARY. The short version of this guide (360 words)
Most books require active, ongoing marketing to grow. However, a rare few will benefit from strong word of mouth and timeless relevance, growing automatically year after year. You want to write the second type.
Instead of trying to figure out how to market whatever book you happened to write, a new book can be intentionally designed and refined toward maximizing word of mouth and back-catalog potential.
All non-fiction is either pleasure-giving (e.g. "interesting", "fascinating", "thought-provoking") or problem-solving (e.g. "life-changing", "skill-building", "decision-clarifying"). Problem-solving books are easy for folks to recommend to anyone with that problem.
Your book also needs to succeed in reliably and convincingly solving the problem it promises to solve. This requires that (1) it contains knowledge which works and that (2) the book is sufficiently well-written for those contents to be understood, extracted, and applied by an average reader.
The first requirement—the effectiveness of the advice—can be confirmed and improved via in-person teaching (either 1-on-1 or with a group). This can be done before a first draft exists, allowing for cheap and rapid iteration of the core message, contents, and tools.
The second requirement—the understandability and accessibility of the book itself—can be confirmed and improved via aggressive use of cohorts of early beta readers, plus a rapid editing cycle on a single live document. Reader feedback is used both qualitatively (via what they tell you) and also quantitatively (tracking where they stop engaging with the document to identify the book's weakest sections).
Beyond problem-solving and readability, word of mouth depends on readers rapidly extracting significant value from the text. The book should therefore be both as short as possible and also front-loaded with value (i.e. don't hold off on the big reveal).
While this approach could be used by both published and self-published authors, it's especially valuable for those interested in maximizing passive income via self-publishing.
That's it. If you already believe (and know how to do) all that, then feel free to stop reading. If you want more justification, information, guidance, or practicalities, then read on.
(And if you're just here for the profit and sales numbers of The Mom Test, they're in the next section.)

CHAPTER 1. What this guide is and isn't, why I wrote it, and who I am
Why I'm a relevant source of advice
⏳
Short version: I'm a product person who wrote a couple books which people seem to enjoy and which currently do $10k/month in pure passive income (and growing). I believe there's a repeatable method to writing this sort of book which is different from what other authors recommend, and which I can teach you to do. This approach is only for non-fiction books such as how-to, skill-building, decision-making, and problem-solving.
Six years ago, I wrote a short book called The Mom Test, about how to run better customer interviews. It earned a humble $535 in its first month and has since grown to about $10k/month in profit. It's now taught at universities like Harvard, MIT, and UCL; is recommended at top startup accelerators like YC and Seedcamp; and is used at companies like SkyScanner and Shopify. It recently hit #1 on Amazon in two of its categories and has been translated by enthusiastic readers into ten languages. This all happened while I was fully ignoring the book and doing essentially zero active marketing.
I self-published, had no editor or marketing help, and launched a book full of typos. Despite all that, the book has been unusually profitable for non-fiction. Across the last six years, it has put just over $300k in my pocket (Amazon only). Benchmarking against industry reports, I seem to be one of the 500 most profitable new authors of the last five years, even competing against folks backed by big publishers. And although I undeniably did get a bit lucky (mostly because what I wanted to write about also happened to be something people wanted to read about), I believe that the bits that worked can be understood and repeated. (I've recently released a second book, The Workshop Survival Guide, which is showing strong early signs, but it's still too soon to judge.)
Here's the graph of lifetime monthly profits for Mom Test's Amazon sales. You'll notice that there was never any big launch. Just steady growth. That's because the book was designed in a very particular way to remain relevant forever while also benefitting from strong word-of-mouth.
To add some extra context to the graph: On average, I earn about $10 profit per copy sold. The paperback earns more ($18 profit per copy), but the ebook sells more ($7 profit per copy). So the y-axis for "books sold per month" should go up to about 1000. If those per-copy profits seem high, see the section (two from now) on self-publishing profitability. Overall, this graph shows sales for about 25k copies. Excluded from this chart/numbers, I've also sold another 10k copies sold in bulk and 15k in other languages (but with very low profits on foreign versions). The audiobook only launched a few months ago and I haven't included it here, but it seems to be adding about 25% to monthly volume and 5% to monthly profitability.
I'd would have loved to wait to release this guide until I had more experience, more books, and more data. But I suspect there's never going to be a perfect time. I'm doing it now because a few of my friends are beginning to write their own books and have asked for help. The advice and approach herein seems to have made their lives easier and their books better, so I'm sharing it more broadly in case it's helpful for you too.
From a reader's perspective, good non-fiction is easy to read and changes their approach to life. From the author's perspective, a good book delivers reliable passive income and grows via word of mouth. But please be aware that this guide isn't a marketing strategy; it's not something you can slap onto any book to make it "viral". Rather, it's a product design strategy; it's about designing and writing a book in a way which is fundamentally easy for readers to talk about and recommend.
A bit of encouragement for folks who don't think they can write a book
⏳
Short version: You probably have a book in you, and you're probably already good enough to do it.
Four reassuring truths for the would-be author:
Firstly, you don't need to be the best at something in order to write the best book about it for some particular type of reader. For example, I didn't write the best book on customer interviews for everyone, but I did write the best book about it for introverted techies. (Which was possible because it is anchored firmly their worldview, speaking their language, and aimed at their precise level of expertise). Most authors try to target their books as broadly as possible, which I think is a mistake. Better to be loved by someone than ignored by everyone.
Secondly, the first draft is supposed to be terrible. Every first draft is dumpster fire. That's okay. All writing begins by being awful and only starts becoming good during rewrites and editing. So if you're getting drepressed about the quality of your first draft, just relax. Of course it's going to be terrible. That's how it begins. Remember that a first draft is still an infant; it's unfair to judge it by grown-up standards.
Thirdly, writing coherent non-fiction is a task of manual labor, not genius. If you're willing to engage with beta readers and invest the hours in editing, you'll eventually scrub through the rust and reveal the value underneath. When people say they're "bad at writing", what they usually mean is that they're unwilling to spend sufficient time on feedback and editing. Yes, it will be slow. But being able to do something slowly is very different from being unable to do it at all.
Lastly, it does take a long time, but perhaps not as long as you might fear. If you can give it an hour or two a day, you'll have a first draft in a couple of weeks, and can then fiddle with it from there. Mom Test took nine months (part-time) between writing the first word and having a finished book on Amazon. (Workshop Survival took longer, but mostly because I became addicted to rewrites.)
There are plenty of bad books out there. I'm not encouraging you to write another paperweight just to tick a box on your CV. But at the intersection of your experiences, and for the audience you understand, and with the willingness to invest the hours in editing, then yes, I believe you can make something great.
Profitability and self-publishing
⏳
Short version: If you care about the income from your book, then you almost certainly want to self-publish. It does require a bit of extra work, but will 5-10x your earnings.
Publishing deals for new authors typically offer about 5-12% royalties, plus an advance of a few grand. (But remember that's an advance, not a payment, so it comes out of your future royalties and is irrelevant from a earnings perspective.)
Self-publishing, on the other hand will get you 60-70% royalties. (The other 30-40% goes to Amazon and printing costs). I use a publisher for my Russian version. The book has been a steady best-seller and is on its fourth print run. For each copy sold, I earn $0.50. For the self-published English version, I earn $10. That's a big difference.
In order for you to simply break even on the deal, a publisher needs to sell 5-10 times as many books as you could have sold on your own.
Well... they can, right? After all, they're publishers. But here's the rub: publishers are risk averse and don't want to blow their budget on an unproven title. As such, a large part of your proposal and negotiations will center around how you are going to promote your book. What?! Isn't marketing their whole thing? Well yes, but only after you, the author, have proven the book's potential by selling at least 10,000 copies on your own.
📕
As a result of the above, publishers love authors who already have a large audience via running a popular popular blog, newsletter, or just being famous. This sort of "author platform" makes the book significantly less risky from the publisher's perspective, which makes them more willing to commit to a guaranteed upfront marketing spend. Depending on the specifics, this might sufficiently improve the attractiveness of their offer. (I'm still skeptical, but it's possible.) But if you aren't already "famous" in this way, then the publisher math is rarely going to work in your favor.
In any case, one way or another, you're on the hook for selling the first 10k copies through either your pre-existing audience or by hustling the launch marketing. From an income perspective, that's a pretty raw deal.
If you're self-published, selling 10k copies puts $70k+ in your pocket. With a publisher? More like $10k (minus your advance). And remember, the publisher won't help you sell the first 10k copies, so you're doing it on your own either way.
For publishing math to really work out, your book needs to exist in a market with the potential for a million copies sold. (Since a publisher selling "only" 100k copies is no better than you selling 10k while self-published.) But most non-fiction markets aren't large enough to even allow for a million readers. As such, the math just doesn't work. (Again, this is for an income-oriented author. If you care more about exposure or impact, then you'd weigh it differently.)
📕
One way to extract more value from publishers is to begin by self-publishing—keeping full royalties from the first 10k copies sold—and then transition to a publishing deal after the book is already proven and you've gained significant negotiating leverage. Osterwalder did this very effectively with Business Model Generation, as did Weinberg with Traction.
Two other huge benefits to self-publishing are brevity and speed. A publisher will require you to hit a certain wordcount (usually 50k), even if that means padding it out with needless anecdotes and fluff. (Ever wonder why so many business books seem like strong core idea surrounded by needless garbage? It's because the publishers require it.) But business books are problem-solving products. Should you be proud that your product requires more of your customers' time before it delivers its benefit? No! Your product should deliver its value as quickly as humanly possible. The Mom Test was only 30k words, and I can't tell you how many busy readers have thanked me for that. But when I was talking to publishers, they told me that I would need to add an extra 20k words to it ("Some more stories, maybe?") just to make it look like a "real" business book.
Speed (to market) is the other profit-influencing factor. Once you're done writing, a publisher will need 6-12 months before the book is up for sale. Self-published, it takes three days (including both digital and paperback). That's up to a year's worth of extra income, reviews, and growth.
I'm not saying that publishers are evil or out to get you. It's just that the deal they offer is no longer the best deal (for most people), and you can do far better from a financial perspective by self-publishing.
If you want to more detail on self-publishing, The Appendix contains some helpful guidance on the tasks and tools of self-pub.
📕
Even some well-established authors are switching to self-publish. Some years back, Barry Eisler turned down a $500k advance in order to self-publish. From an interview: "Getting half a million bucks and 14.9% royalties isn't as lucrative as no money up front and 70% royalties forever... You need to move five times the volume at 14.9%."
👨
Speaking personally, I've chosen to use publishers for foreign-language versions like Serbian, Russian, and Iranian where I'm unable to create the initial word of mouth on my own. (And they're willing to promote it since the English version is already proven.)
With that out of the way, let's get into the question of designing and writing a good book.

CHAPTER 2. The nature and nuance of recommendable books
The core "problem" of publishing is that lots of books already exist, making it hard for a new book to get noticed and bought. They're also unsure about which books have potential to grow into best-sellers, which forces them to take a "wait and see" approach. A better approach fixes both these problems.
The core "mistake" of authors is to write a book about whatever seems interesting, and to then try to figure out how to sell it. But your choice of what to write and how to write it will fundamentally change your book's potential in the marketplace, so you must consider these questions early.
The "normal" approach: build an author platform
⏳
Short version: Most indie authors build a mailing list via ongoing content marketing and then sell books to an audience who already trusts them. I do it differently.
As mentioned in the previous section, it's hugely helpful to have an "author platform" (i.e. an audience of potential readers who already trust you). Some people already have one via their life's previous activities, and some authors choose to build a new one from scratch alongside writing their book. But I don't want to.
Let's not mince words: building an author platform will make your book more successful. The problem is that it's not a one-time task; it's an ongoing activity which you start doing today (or yesterday, ideally) and continue doing for the next ten years.
For me, that's a dealbreaker. I don't want another job. That's part of why I'm so keen on designing books that can thrive off word of mouth alone. And I don't really mind that I'm being "suboptimal". As long as my income is sufficient to pay for my life (which it is), I'm more motivated to maximize my leisure time than my earnings.
But listen: if you're up for the work of building a list of enthusiastic potential readers, then you should do it. It will undoubtedly make you money, and it will save you from the fuss of having to figure out how to create your initial audience to seed the word of mouth (which we'll return to later in the book).
👨
I used to quite enjoy blogging, so I ended up with a small author platform (about 5k subscribers) as a side-effect of that hobby. And it did make things easier. But as my interests have shifted in subsequent years, I ended up abandoning blogging and deleting my list. I now have exactly 70 subscribers (who apparently want my personal updates for some unfathomable reason). I mention this simply to reassure you that I'm not hiding some secret advantage or asset which is causing my books to grow. They're growing because of how they're designed, not because of who I am. (Whatever "fame" I now have is a result of the books' success and not other way round.)
My approach is a little different (although having an author platform is hugely helpful, so feel free to do that also, if you like). But before we get into it, we need to change the way we think about books.
📚
If you do want to build an author platform, don't take advice from authors; they're miles behind the cutting edge. Books as pleasure-givers vs. problem-solvers
⏳
Short version: Some books deliver pleasure, whereas others solve sharp, important problems. You want to write a problem-solver.
When someone buys a book, they're doing it for one of two reasons:
1.
Because they expect it to bring them pleasure
2.
Because they expect it to solve a problem in their life
At first glance, this appears to be a division between fiction and non-fiction. But it isn't.
A lot of non-fiction is actually "infotainment". These books don't solve any problems or change the readers' lives, but simply offer some interesting stories or examples or drama or information. For example, my upstairs neighbor wrote a serious, heavily-researched book about the consequences of endlessly pursuing growth (for companies, governments, etc.). It's well-written and interesting. But what problem does it solve for the reader? Unless the reader is in the (extremely small) niche of open-minded politicians trying to use real data to inform their policy decisions, then this book isn't solving any problem at all. Despite being a serious book on a serious topic, it will read for the "pleasure" of learning and thinking.
Publishing infotainment is a terrible business for the simple reason that pleasure is a commodity. If your book provides pleasure, then it is interchangable with any other source of pleasure. Is anyone going to seek out your book in particular? Probably not, unless it has been recommended to them by name. But who is going to recommend a book which was just "interesting"? Who am I going to recommend my neighbor's book to? I've had it on my shelf for two years and haven't loaned it to a single person.
Whereas there are other books which I've bought ten copies of since I loan them out so frequently. Those are the problem-solvers. That's the sort of book you want to write.
Problem-solvers allow for supercharged word of mouth
⏳
Short version: If a potential reader has an important, painful problem, and if your book convincingly solves that problem, then someone will go out of their way to recommend your book to them.
Here's how The Mom Test gets discovered:
A new entrepreneur is starting a business. Everyone is telling them to "go talk to their customers and learn what they care about." So they try it. But it's harder than it sounded. Maybe the conversations are awkward, or the data isn't trustworthy, or maybe they can't even find anyone to talk to.
And then, in their next meeting with an advisor or friendly entrepreneur, they start talking about how hard it has been. And the other person says, "Oh, I know the solution to your problem. The solution is this book. It's called The Mom Test."
That's how a problem-solving book behaves. Is that person going to buy a different book instead? Hell no. Will care if it's twice as expensive as other books? Nope. Are they going to let it sit unread on the shelf? No way. Because this book is the solution to an urgent and important problem in their life.
When your book solves an important problem, it stops behaving like a low-value commodity and starts behaving like a valuable must-have. It starts to become actively recommended and urgently sought out.
Writing this sort of book is the foundation of making money from publishing. And almost nobody does it properly.
👨
You may have noticed that The Mom Test is a dumb title for a book about doing customer interviews. My thinking was that, since the book was a problem-solver, it would be recommended through word-of-mouth rather than "discovered" through search or browsing Amazon. As such, I wanted a name which was easy to remember and say rather than one which was descriptive or easy to understand. (Of course, it's possible that the book could have done even better with a better name, but we'll never know.)
Folks tend to be fairly imprecise when thinking about word of mouth. The popular belief is that, if you write something sufficiently good, then people will recommend it. This is wrong.
Word of mouth is predictable and measurable
For non-fiction, a book's "recommendability" only partly depends on the quality of the book. It also depends on the book's promise (i.e. which problem it solves) and how exposed your audience is to recommendations.
To estimate its word of mouth potential, score each of these three factors from 0-10:
1.
Depth of problem: How important and urgent is the problem you are promising to solve? How painful is it if it remains unsolved? How motivated are people to fix it?
2.
Audience exposure to advice: When someone begins to have the problem which is fixed by your book, how quickly do they either seek out or receive advice or recommendations about it?
3.
Book quality: How good is the book itself (primarily in terms of its ability to convincingly solve the problem, and secondarily as an enjoyable, readable, beautiful object)?
(Ignore #3 for now—we'll return to it as the core goal for most of the rest of this guide.)
For now, you can just multiply together the first two numbers to get a 0-100 measure of roughly how quickly your book will be able to spread through its target audience via recommendations.
The Mom Test was an 8/10 on depth of problem and a 10/10 on the audience's exposure to advice. (Tech entrepreneurs spend a ton of time reading articles and also receive loads of advice from peers, mentors, and investors.) So The Mom Test's potential for word of mouth is sitting pretty at 80/100.
The Workshop Survival Guide, on the other hand, was solving a less urgent problem (5) for a far less advice-exposed audience (3). It's probably fair to say that it's only a 15/100. Whoops.
Interestingly, although Workshop Survival is better-written and better as a book than Mom Test (say a 10/10 compared to Mom Test's 7/10), that still leaves Workshop lagging at 150/1000 compared to Mom Test's 540.
Note that this is not predicting the total number of books sold. (Total sales will shift based on author platform, fame, luck, active marketing, and total audience size.) What it is predicting is how quickly your book can spread from one reader to another via word of mouth.
Importantly, these numbers are multiplied, not summed. A zero in any of the three categories will zero your whole book. Which brings us to the harsh truth for the income-oriented author:
No matter how good your book, an unimportant problem or inaccessible audience will obliterate its potential.
But there's good news, too:
By adjusting the scope of your book (i.e. which problem it solves) and who you write toward (i.e. the audience), you can massively boost its potential with essentially zero effort.
You get to pick your readers. And you get to pick your topic. As mentioned in the intro, you (probably) won't succeed if you make a purely cynically decision and chase the money by writing about something you don't care about. But there's surely a bit of intersection between the things you do care about and the things which have high-potential for word of mouth, right?

Bestsellers vs. back catalog
⏳
Short version: 90% of the money in books isn't from the heavily-marketed new titles, but from the older books which have become classics and continue to spread via word-of-mouth (i.e. they are in the back catalog). You should intentionally write for the back catalog.
Did you know that all the bestseller lists are a lie? They're a lie because they only list new books. But it turns out that 90% of the money from books comes from those old classics which people keep buying, year after year, because those books are the best way to solve an important problem.
"Bestsellers" are temporary. They spike early because of the marketing weight behind them. But marketing is hard. And when the marketing stops, so do the sales. The publishing industry doesn't mind, because they've got another thousand authors lined up to take their shot. But if you're writing for yourself, that's no good. You don't want a job in marketing; you want to write a book that sells itself on auto-pilot.
Book publishers make more than 90% of their profit from books they published more than six months ago. And yet they put 2% of their effort into promoting and selling those books. —Seth Godin, Understanding the backlist
"Back catalog" (or backlist) is a book industry term for books which remain relevant for many years, and which people keep buying and reading. People tend to pay attention to the flashy new bestsellers, but that's not where the money is. The money is in writing something which people will always need to read.
The Mom Test is now a back catalog book, which is great: sales grow each month instead of shrinking, and I don't need to do any marketing to keep it that way.
So how can we make it into the back catalog? There are two requirements (plus many optional strategies). First, the content needs to be timeless (as opposed to temporary and trendy). Second, there needs to be some way for people to hear about it without you needing to continually market it to them.
This gets us to the next two pillars: writing "word of mouth problems solvers".
"Word of mouth" means that people are going to go out of their way to recommend your book to other people. But why would they do that? I believe business books get recommended when the book solves a sharp pain which you know the other person is experiencing. For example, if you sit down in a meeting with an entrepreneur who is struggling to understand their customers, it's very natural to recommend The Mom Test.
But what's the moment for recommending a more generic business bestseller? What's the sharp pain being solved which creates a moment of easy recommendation? Books like that need to be on bookstore shelves; since nobody is recommending them to each other, potential readers need to see and impulse buy them. Whereas The Mom Test never needs to be on store shelves, since people have been recommended it for their current problem and will then search for it by name. That's what I mean by the third pillar, which is that the book should be a "problem solver".
The most powerful business books aren't actually books at all; they are problem-solving B2B products with a low, one-time price point, zero customer support, and global distribution via Amazon.
So don't just "write a book". Instead, design it just like any other B2B product by finding a sharp problem which people will pay to have solved. And then solve it with a book. Designing your book around a sharp problem is how you create the opportunities for word of mouth. And, alongside your choice of timeless content, that's how you get into the back catalog.
📕
An alternative, if you have the network, is to write it as an introductory textbook. This loosens the requirement of needing a sharp problem and depending on word of mouth, but will take significantly more work to get started. You don't need to cover a whole semester (that's always going to be the domain of the big publishers), but can instead aim for it to support just one or two lectures. (Although writing a good problem-solver can get you into universities for free, as happened with The Mom Test.)
Why the marketing stunt and the book tour are bad strategy
⏳
Short version: Unless your book already benefits from strong word-of-mouth, marketing stunts will only deliver a temporary spike in sales and are rarely worth the time.
Book tours are low-value unless the book solves is able to leverage from word of mouth. This sort of hands-on marketing is high-cost and low-volume, which means that the handful of people in the room are only valuable if they're the beginning of going viral.
I'm not saying you should never do a book tour or one-off marketing stunt. I'm just saying that it won't save a cause a book to suddenly "make it". If your book has strong word-of-mouth potential, then sure, it's as good a way as any to create the initial word of mouth. But remember that the goal is to seed an audience for a self-perpetuating product, and not to go out yourself and sell things. (Which means that giving away books at these events is potentially wiser than selling them.)
The above logic also applies to podcasts, conference talks, and any other "one shot" promotional efforts. Unless your product has inherent virality (i.e. it's easy for people to recommend), then don't bother.
👨
I really believed in the idea of a book tour, so I tested it fairly thoroughly. I gave talks on The Mom Test at a dozen large conferences (with audiences of 500-2000 at each), along with teaching 100+ workshops to audiences of 30-50 (workshops were my day job at the time). While the data isn't bulletproof, the result was proved to my own satisfaction by giving each audience a trackable discount link to the book, and also watching for spikes in Amazon sales. The net effect on book sales was roughly zero. (The exception is when the conference buys books in bulk and gives them to every attendee. This is a wonderful deal and I do it whenever possible.)
Amazon ads can drive plenty of traffic, but your results will vary based on how "impulse buyable" it is. If your book would thrive in a train station bookstore, then it will probably do okay with ads. My limited experience (two books) has made me suspect that it's fairly easy to get an extra €200-500/month for most decent books via ads, but would be a very rare book indeed which could advertise its way to an extra €1k+. (Plus, optimizing the ads requires a fair number of hours per week, so the income can hardly be considered "passive" at that point.)
📕
There's also a ton of advice about making money from writing fiction. I don't think it translates to non-fiction. For example, many fiction authors focus churning out multi-book series on the basis that if someone buys one, they'll buy the rest. (This has been taken to an amusing extreme by the popular authors' group 20 books to 50k, which reasons that if your book can earn a humble $7 per day in sales, then you can achieve a living salary of $50k by simply writing 19 more books. Although the math does work out, I suspect that there's an easier way.)
Summary of the two viable strategies
If you want to treat business books as an income source, I think there are two viable strategies:
1.
Treat the book as the product by writing (1) back catalog (2) word of mouth (3) problem solvers.
2.
Treat yourself as the product by building an author platform and a mailing list, and then writing for your audience.
The "hard part" of the first approach is to find a sharp problem and write something which solves it convincingly.
The "hard part" of the second is to put in the consistency to nurture and grow a list over many years.
Either way, you're going to want to self-publish since traditional publishers aren't likely to sell the 10x copies needed to break even on the lower royalties.
👨
A big part of why I prefer the first approach is that I'm bad at showing up every day with steady consistency, whereas I enjoy working in intense bursts and then moving on to something else. But if you're more together than I am, an author platform is a big benefit, even if you follow my core word of mouth strategy. I'm not entirely sure how the platform works if your interests change and you start writing about new stuff (like when I wrote about education instead of entrepreneurship). I asked Ryan Holiday, who has handled it very successfully, and he replied succinctly: "You want to build up a fan base that follows you. That’s the best kind of platform."

CHAPTER 3. Applying startup fundamentals to verify the book's core offering
If you believe that a book is a problem-solving product, then you need to test and refine it just like you would any other product: exposed to and in contact with real customers. This will lead to a better book.
(If you're excited about writing something now, then feel free to thrash out a Table of Contents and/or first draft at any point. Just don't get sucked into editing and perfecting it until you're sure that it's worth writing.)
Testing a book and learning from readers
⏳
Short version: Entrepreneurs have learned that they're far more likely to succeed if they start learning from real customers as early as possible. Authors can (and should) do the same by using a modifyied set of learning tools to answer four key questions.
Authors and publishers could learn a thing or two from the startup community. The single biggest lesson of the last 30 years of global entrepreneurship was a hard one to learn, although it sounds fairly obvious when you write it down:
If you build your whole product in secret, and only show it to customers once it's mostly finished, then you're very likely to have missed the mark in some important way, but without the time and resources to fix it.
The corollary is:
When building a new product, you should get real customers involved in the creation process as early as possible, even while you're still embarrassed by how half-baked it is.
People who really care about what you're doing won't mind the mess. And if they won't engage with the mess, then they probably don't care enough about the underlying problem to engage with the finished product either. This is a helpful way to avoid working for ages on a book that nobody wants to read.
In the context of books-as-a-product, you'll face four major "questions" or "risks":
1.
Problem: Do readers care enough about the problem to even bother trying to learn about and solve it? (You can skip this one if you already understand your readers.)
2.
Solution in a vacuum: Have I found some process/tools/knowledge/etc which convincingly solves that problem?
3.
Solution in a book: Am I able to translate that process/tools/knowledge/etc into book form in a way which still succeeds?
4.
Readability: Can I polish and perfect the book (including both its words and its presentation) to be easy and pleasurable to engage with and learn from?
The first two questions can be dealt with before you've even written a single word. The third requires a manuscript, beta readers, and relentless editing. The fourth requires only craft and patience.
Most authors skip ahead to deal with question four, while wilfully ignoring the first three. This is a mistake, although I do understand why they do it. After all, it's scary to risk learning that nobody cares about the problem (and by extension, your book). But I think it's preferable to discover that harsh truth sooner rather than later. You're then free to choose whether to write your book as a zero-income hobby project, or whether to adjust the scope until it's something that people actually want to buy, read, and recommend.
It's no help to write a beautifully crafted book about something nobody cares about. You will not be rewarded for your hard work or martyrdom. You will be only rewarded for the value you bring to your readers' lives.
I use four main tools for learning about my readers and answering these questions:
1.
Conversations with potential readers about how they're already dealing with it
2.
In-person consulting/coaching/teaching to deliver the solution by hand
3.
Beta readers (in cohorts, on a single live document with rapid editing) to weed out problems with content and structure
4.
Obsessive editing to make it readable and compelling
Some of this stuff is going to seem extra work, but in reality, you do it alongside your writing. And the insight gathered will reduce the numbers of revisions required, so it actually ends up saving time rather than costing it. (It also makes it vastly more likely that you'll be able to write a book that people actually want to buy read.)
Verifying that the problem matters
⏳
Short version: If potential readers aren't already aware of and/or motivated to fix their problem, then they're unlikely to find, buy, read, and recommend your book.
You probably know whether the problem matters. You'll just need to pause and have an explicit think about it.
A problem is "hot" if people are (1) already trying to fix it by (2) actively spending time, money, or attention. They could be trying to fix it right now, or they may have attempted to fix it in the past and then abandoned the effort out of vast frustration—either way is fine. Or, alternately, a problem can matter if it's (1) causing active harm (e.g. financial, psychological) which they're (2) aware of and (3) motivated to fix.
Not every problem matters; just because a problem exists (or even gets complained about) doesn't mean folks are motivated to do anything about it.
But as mentioned, you probably already sufficiently understand your audience and can skip ahead to step two: verifying that the solution works.
📚
If you aren't sure whether the problem matters or how to talk to people and find out, you might want to grab a copy of The Mom Test.
☑️
Do this now: Write down your book's value proposition and who it's for. This could be the title/subtitle, or it could just be a private note to yourself. Is it solving an important problem for a group of people who care? Could you make the problem more "hot" by shifting either who it's for or what it's promising to do for them?
Verifying that the solution works
⏳
Short version: Before putting the process/knowledge/etc into a book, try teaching it to a few people in-person. If nobody is excited to get free help like this, then beware! This allows you to iterate and refine your content without being slowed down by the tedious task of writing.
A book is built of words and pages. But within those pages is some sort of knowledge. People aren't buying your book because it's a pretty book; they're buying it for what it contains. They're buying a solution to their problems. From your perspective as an author, this simple fact has year-saving implications.
That readers are paying for a solution rather than a book means you can verify whether (1) people want the solution and (2) whether the solution works, before the book is written.
How? By sitting down with them—in person—and giving them the help that they would have gotten from your book.
What if nobody wants to sit down with you? Well, then it's very likely that this particular person doesn't care very much about the problem you're solving. This could mean that you're talking to the wrong type of person (i.e. your readerbase is actually a different sort of person than you thought) or it could be because the problem doesn't matter.
Now, you aren't going to try to trick people into sitting down with you. This isn't a sales pitch. And you aren't going to call in favors and beg them to sit down with you. That wouldn't tell you whether they care. Rather, you're going to offer to help them accomplish something which is important to them. And you're doing it not because they owe you, but because it's mutually beneficial. You get some reader insight and they get their problem solved.
These conversations serve two purposes. Firstly, they're a canary in the coalmine which offers early warning if you're heading toward a flop.
If nobody wants to sit down with you and have their problem solved—for free and in-person—then nobody is going to buy your book. You don't need to give up, but you do need to change something.
Secondly, you'll be able to figure out which bits of your vast knowledge are most relevant and helpful. Which metaphors work best and most easily? Which tools are the most impactful and helpful? Which tools take far too long to explain and probably aren't worth the effort? Which stories and anecdotes hold their attention, and which cause them to glance at their phones?
The learning you get from these 1-on-1 coaching sessions translates directly into a better table of contents and a better book.
Whenever you learn what works (and doesn't) for your readers, you'll want to update your "product". Updating a whole book is a pain in the ass, and costs so much time that you will only be able to do a couple iterations. Whereas adjusting the advice you're giving between meetings only takes a few minutes. This allows you to rapidly iterate, and to continue doing so until you're absolutely certain that your offering can make good on its promise. That means getting early feedback using meetings instead of manuscripts.
Before I wrote the first words of The Mom Test, I had already helped dozens of entrepreneurs to solve the "problem" of fixing their customer interviews, first in friendly 1-on-1s and later in group workshops. (I also wrote a book about running great workshops, if you're interested.) So although there were still plenty of uncertainties about the final book as a product, I was able to write it safe in the convinction that (1) the problem mattered and (2) the solution worked.
🤷
Can you really get honest feedback during these meetings? Won't the other person be biased? Well, it depends what you do with your time together. If you pitch your idea and ask for feedback, then yes, it's going to be biased, worthless, and misleading. So instead, don't even mention your book. Just help them solve their problem and see what works for them. Stop thinking "feedback from asking" and start thinking "insight from helping and watching".
👨
For The Mom Test, I also put up a little website and started pre-selling the book (with no discount, refunds, or guarantee that I would ever finish it). A hundred people bought it for $30, which put three grand in my pocket and was obviously nice. But it's hardly a required step and doesn't really "test" anything other than that you happen to be able to drive relevant traffic to a website. By all means do it if it's easy for you, but you shouldn't confuse the result with real learning. Landing pages and pre-sales are about marketing and cashflow, not learning or product. After all, you can promise anything you want on a landing page, but that doesn't mean the final book will actually deliver on it.
☑️
Do this now: Write a list of the people you know (or even know of) who could benefit from what you're trying to offer. Rank them by friendiness and/or accessibility. Send the first few a message saying you're doing some research for a book on how folks get through this problem, and that you'd would love to sit down and help them make some progress on it, if it's still important to them. (Or even just ask them about how they've already dealt with it.) If nobody wants to agree, then pause to have a serious think about why not. If a few people do agree, use what you learn to nail down the soon-to-be book's core content.

CHAPTER 4. Write the first and second drafts
At this point, you basically know that the book is worth writing, and that if you write it well, people will receive value from it. You haven't guaranteed success, but you have massively de-risked the whole ordeal.
It's now invest the time to write a first draft. And then—almost always—throwing it away and writing a second. I'll share a few words about writing the drafts, and we'll then jump into the meatier and more important question or how to use beta readers to make it good.
Set aside your critical mind and slam down a first draft
⏳
Short version: Having learned as much as is possible before writing an actual book, it's time to create the first and second drafts. The first draft is for thinking, and the second is to prepare for feedback.
Before you get stressed out, I want to reassure you that nobody will see the first draft.
The first draft is for you. The second draft is for your beta readers. The third is for your actual readers. So don't worry if the first draft is a hot mess or you feel like you're writing garbage. It's just to help you think.
The first draft is more about helping yourself think than it is about creating an actual book. Once you've dumped whatever's in your brain into something which is vaguely book-shaped, you can then throw it away and start over (or rewrite what you've got, if that seems easier) to get something which is fit for consumption by a few brave beta readers.
In general, it's helpful to work from a Table of Contents. Write that first, in as much detail as possible. Then expand the headings and subheadings into paragraphs. Some authors like to work linearly, progressing from start to finish, whereas others jump around the Table of Contents, expanding random sections in whatever order strikes their fancy. (I did the former in my previous books, whereas I'm finding the latter more natural for this one.)
If you find yourself second-guessing yourself too much, then avoid reading what you've written. Just write. Or follow Hemingway's ego-busting advice to "write drunk, edit sober."
However you do it, just get the words down.
🔗
For more tips on the writing process (writer's block, creativity, daily habits, etc.), check out the Appendix section on creativity and the writing process.
☑️
Do this now: Write a detailed Table of Contents, expand it into a fast-and-loose first draft, and then overhaul that into a semi-readable and semi-coherent second draft. (Yes, this step might take a while.)
Rewrite with an eye toward your beta readers
⏳
Short version: Put your effort into clarifying the core sections. Delete or skip any optional extras, at least for now.
The first draft is usually such a mess that there's no point sending it to beta readers. Instead, you'll want to rewrite or edit what you've got into something which is a bit more coherent and palatable. You don't need to worry about typos, but you do need to ensure that the core message exists and is understandable.
It's fine to skip writing the optional extras for now. Planning a big Appendix? Skip it. Optional chapter with a hands-on tutorial? If it's not core, you can do it later. The part that matters, at this point, is to ensure that readers can understand and gain value from your core message. Spend your time polishing those parts of the book, and (temporarily) leave out any optional extras. Leaving them in will just reduce your editing iteration speed and will draw the beta reader's attention away from where you need it most.
Prepare for feedback by moving the manuscript to the appropriate tool
⏳
Short version: You can write a first draft using whatever you want, but you'll need to switch to something with easy commenting before you start doing your feedback and editing. Google Docs is a good option.

As far as actual options, Google Docs is the main contender (setting the permissions to allow folks to comment but not edit), and is what I used for The Workshop Survival Guide. I'm writing this guide in Notion, which also seems nice.

I really love the early part of the editing process where beta readers are heavily involved. The manuscript morphs into a sort of community hub where folks are commenting on each other's comments and getting into debates about what works and doesn't. It's great fun and I find that the early reader engagement provides tons of motivation for me to keep writing. (So if you're reading this as a beta reader, get involved and leave some comments!)

CHAPTER 5. Writing a good book — beta readers and qualitative/quantitative book feedback
"Beta readers" are not proofreaders, spellcheckers, editors, or anyone you pay. They are actual readers, allowed early access to a book they want to read in exchange for transparency about what they love and hate and about where they get confused and bored. This is hugely valuable and strangely uncommon. There's a bit of nuance to handling them properly which is important to get right.
Verifying that it works as a book using beta readers
⏳
Short version: Instead of hiring professional editors, send early drafts to batches of potential readers using a tool where they can easily comment, and where you can see how they're engaging with the draft. Do this in cohorts, with a few new readers for each major update (e.g. weekly).
As a bit of backstory: For my second book, I experimented with hiring a couple expert freelancers: an editor and marketing consultant. (Both were very capable and I had fun working with them, but neither was necessary and I wouldn't pay for the help again since it was rather expensive.) The reason I mention them is because each had spent 10+ years in the big publishing houses before going freelance, which meant that they had seen how top-tier publishers make books.
And when they first opened my manuscript, they were shocked to see that every page was annotated with non-stop reader comments along the right margin, like this:
The subsequent conversations went like this:
"Who are all these people leaving comments? I thought you hadn't had it edited or proofread yet — where is all this feedback coming from?"
I replied, "Oh, they're the beta readers. You know, to make sure that the book can actually do what it claims to be able to do. And to figure out where folks get confused."
And they said, "But they've left hundreds of comments! How did you get them to do that??"
"I didn't really do anything. I just found people who wanted what the book was offering. And if they stop leaving comments before they reach the end, then I know the book's not good enough yet. So I look at where their comments stop and try to make that part better."
And it blew their minds. Coming from a product background, and thinking of my book as a problem-solving product, the idea of having beta readers seemed like a no-brainer. But apparently, this is not done in the publishing industry. Now, publishers do pay plenty of professionals to read it: content editors, copy editors, line editors, proofreaders, and even sensitivity readers. But no actual readers. Wow.
Still, it's not enough to just email out a manuscript and ask for feedback. To receive valuable insight, you need to get a few things right:
1.
Recruit beta readers who are identical to your actual readers
2.
Prime them with instructions and guidelines to encourage useful feedback
3.
Invite them to the manuscript in cohorts, with a few fresh readers each time you make major changes (i.e. each week or two)
4.
Understand the meaning of both the qualitative and quantative data of the comments
5.
Pick a writing tool which supports the above (which we discussed earlier)
Some of these tasks are more obvious than others, so I hope you'll forgive me for jumping around a bit to deal with the more interesting bits first.
Interpreting beta reader feedback - quantitative
You'll get two main types of data from your beta readers:
1.
The actual contents of the comments themselves (qualitative)
2.
Where those comments stop (quantitative)
It's fairly obvious that the comments themselves contain valuable feedback. But what's equally useful is to see where those comments stop. Because remember: the beta readers represent your actual readers. They are people who want to get the benefit promised by your book. And if they're giving up early, then it means your actual readers will also give up early. And if you're aiming toward strong word of mouth, then readers need to finish your book.
As a key metric, you want to know how much of your book the beta readers are getting through before getting bored, as well as where the exact sections where you're losing them. You measure this by looking for where their comments stop.
This gives you an at-a-glance measure of how readable and compelling your manuscript currently is, while also highlighting specific problem areas. Those are the sections which need heavy editing.
Once you see most of your readers finishing most of the book, then you can feel confident that your core message is no longer being undermined by dull or incoherent writing.

This step also provides a nice double-check that your book is solving an important problem for people who care. If people will only read your manuscript as a favor to you (as opposed to because they urgently want to), then it's a pretty good sign that you're writing a book which isn't solving a hot problem. Folks don't need to finish it to prove that they care, but they should at least be fairly eager to take a look.
🤷
Just like any other metric, this one loses its value if you try to optimize it at the expense of common sense and context. For example, a book containing only a single sentence would presumably achieve a 100% completion rate, but is still unlikely to succeed. The book must first be desirable, then effective, and only then readable.
🤖
For the startup folks: A book is a single-use product with a viral loop, with the text of the book itself acting as onboarding friction. You get paid upfront by new readers when they decide that they care about the problem, but they don't fully activate (and become capable of making referrals) until they've reached the end (or at least extracted the core value). If the product is either ineffective (i.e. doesn't do what it says on the tin) or high-friction (i.e. difficult to read), then they'll abandon before receiving value, eliminating their potential as a referrer and clobbering your viral coefficient. You can therefore boost growth by improving any point in the book-as-a-funnel, including (1) sharper problem, (2) better solution, or (3) better writing. That's why we spend so much time using beta readers to search for high-friction sections—the entire manuscript is part of our viral loop and every section needs to be optimized toward that end. Fortunately, although the product (i.e. book) is single-use, happy readers remain as a referrer forever and will continue to "invite" new customers for many years. As such, the coefficient for a sharp book can be far greater than 1.0, although you won't actually see it hockey stick since the referral loop happens over months instead of days.

The most emotionally difficult type of feedback is when someone starts giving criticism which very clearly reveals that they were not paying attention earlier in the book.
When this happens, it is enormously tempting to dismiss their comment by replying with something like, "Please review Chapter 2. It's covered there." And before a book can be glowingly reviewed and lovingly recommended, it needs to be enjoyed and understood. Which means improving it until it works for even the slightly distracted, slightly rushed, slightly less-than-perfect readers.
(You'll never fully achieve this goal, by the way. Some folks are just impossibly distracted. But by aiming toward it as an ideal, you'll end up with a better book.)

I knew I was about to make some bad decisions, so I closed the document and took a vacation. Upon my return, I dug in and realized that they were non-native English speakers and that I had been overrelying on aphorisms and flowery language early in the book to deliver core concepts. I simplified the language and the confusion stopped.
Finding and recruiting beta readers
⏳
Short version: Tell people what you're working on. If they're into it, invite them to review an early version. If they're not into it, leave them be.
Not everyone you invite is going to accept your invitation, and not everyone who accepts is going to engage with the manuscript. For The Workshop Survival Guide, I found probably 40 people who said they wanted to be beta readers. Roughly 25 of them actually opened the document and left at least one comment, and about 15 engaged significantly (i.e. dozens of comments and many hours invested). So roughly a third of my "qualified leads" ended up "converting" into real beta readers (40→25→15). But that was enough.
So where does one find these magically helpful people? The answer is the same way you find folks who share a hobby. For example:
Let's say you want to start organizing a poker night. This requires a few people. Maybe you know one or two already. Great, tell them about the game.
You also might also ask some friends if they know anyone else who is friendly, available, and keen on this particular hobby. If so, you've got a couple more. You're off to the races.
Some of your early players are likely to know others. That's just how life works. So as long as you've put together an enjoyable game, they're likely to make a couple introductions.
Also, when you get chatting with random people—say at a friendly gathering, business event, or meetup—you might ask them if they're into poker. If so, you can easily say that you're organizing a game and ask if they'd like to join.
But what if you talk to someone who doesn't especially like poker? Should you try to convince them to come to your game anyway? No, obviously not. You're trying to find folks who are interested, not to strongarm folks who aren't.
That's it. It works the same for beta readers.
(Alternately, if you happen to have an author platform and/or audience, then you can just offer to give folks early access in exchange for their feedback. This is obviously very convenient, but you needn't rely on it.)
Look, it's like all of marketing: take advantage whatever helpful resources you happen to have at your disposal (audience, fame, personal network, etc.), but don't waste time getting hung up on the injustice of the world if you need to do it the hard way.
For The Workshop Survival Guide, one of the best beta readers was a random dude I met at a wedding who used to be a high school teacher. Another was a consultant I got chatting to at a Barcelona meetup. Another was someone I saw on Instagram who was talking about teaching, so I cold-messaged them. Back when I was writing The Mom Test, I already knew lots of entrepreneurs and mentors, so it was just a matter of sending out some emails.
The world is full of people, and you'll find a few of the right ones easily enough once you get into the habit of scanning for them. (With the caveat that if you're writing about a sensitive topic, you may prefer to recruit readers anonymously from a relevant online forum. I always prefer in-person contacts when possible, but for some topics there's no real choice.)
📕
Business Model Generation took recruitment of beta readers to the extreme by actually charging people to become one. Several dozen folks each paid between $10 and $400 for the priviledge of reviewing early versions and contributing feedback (becoming more expensive as the book approached launch). The publishing industry pays for specialized professionals; the self-publisher gets paid by their fans.
For feedback to be useful, you need to use it
⏳
Short version: Getting good feedback means doing heavy rewrites and deletions, and that's a good thing.
Spending the time on reader feedback won't guarantee that anyone will actually find or buy the book. But it does at least guarantee that the book is understandable and able to reliably deliver the benefits promised on the cover. And that's not nothing.
Of course, once you start getting deep, meaty feedback, you'll need to start making deep, meaty changes. Proofreading is about changing sentences, but beta readers are about changing whole chapters. I threw away and rewrote the entire manuscript of The Mom Test three times. And probably more for The Workshop Survival Guide.
In both cases, once I thought I was "finished", reader feedback helped me find and delete an extra 30,000-50,000 words (including entire chapters) which were adding more distraction than value.
While complete perfection is an unobtainable goal, you do need to ensure the product works. And toward that goal, don't fear the rewrite. It gets better (and easier) every time.

CHAPTER 6. Structuring a book for rapid value extraction
Your beta readers will help improve points and paragraphs, but don't have the high-level view necessary to help you structure and organize the entire manuscript. Thankfully, we have a few guiding principles.
Before a reader can begin recommending your book to others, they need to have received the value themselves. Furthermore, they will only continue "spending" their time on it if the book continues "paying" them back by delivering value. This means:
1.
Your book will be better and more profitable if it's shorter (but not too short, obviously)
2.
Front-load the pay-off such that a reader gets most of the value from the start of the book, and avoid requiring a reader to finish the whole thing to unlock any value at all
3.
Maintain reader goodwill with regularly-spaced "a-ha moments" (i.e. learning outcomes)
Length is a liability
⏳
Short version: Non-fiction readers value brevity. Beyond being "cheaper" (in terms of time) for them to consume, a shorter book is also cheaper for you to create and perfect, making for a more profitable product.
While readers of fiction might enjoy getting lost in a thick tome, readers of non-fiction do not. They appreciate brevity. They are buying and reading your book for the value it offers, and every word you include is delaying their access to that promised value. Imagine your reader is a freelancer who bills at €100/hour. They buy your book for €20 and spend four hours reading it. Your book "cost" €420. If you can deliver the same value in half as many words, then it only "costs" €220. That's a massive savings for the reader, and they'll value your book all the more for it.
👨
Incidentally, this is why I initially priced The Mom Test at $30 instead of a more "normal" $12 or $15. I figured that the bulk of the cost being paid by "professional" readers was their time, so I could double my profits while only marginally increasing their costs. (I'm not suggesting that everyone do this, just explaining my thinking at the time.)
The second-to-last draft of The Mom Test, which I sent to a dozen beta readers, was 70k words. The final book was 30k. Based on reader feedback, I deleted more 40k words (i.e. more than half). Afterwards, the book was vastly easier to write and also vastly better.
The same happened again with The Workshop Survival guide. A "nearly" final version was 120k words. None of the beta readers made it to the end. I deleted 70k words and the final version shipped at a wordcount of 50k.
Also, longer books cost more (mostly in time and slightly in money) to create. The time for writing and editing scales (worse than) linearly with word count. Whereas the sale price of a book is completely independent of wordcount. Short isn't easy, but it is valuable—to both you and your reader.
Deciding what to delete by tightening the customer segment
⏳
Short version: By taking a strong stand about exactly who your book is for, you can determine what they already know and believe, which allows you to leave stuff out. This creates a book dense with value and easy to recommend.
Alright, so short is good for both readers and author. But the book still needs to succeed in delivering whatever it promised on the cover, so you can't just gut it indiscriminately. How to decide what's in and what's out?
Here's a bit of startup 101: if you try to serve every type of customer when you first launch your product or business, then you're almost guaranteed to fail. Let's say you have a product which might be useful for universities or government or teachers. Although the core product and use case is exactly the same, each of these groups needs slightly different features around the edges. Governments need a paper trail and data protection guarantees. Universities need support for teams and roles. Teachers need a low price point and quick setup.
The trap that every new entrepreneur falls into is seeing each of these customer segments as free money. They can't bring themselves to "lose out" on any of those potential users. After all, 90% of the product is perfect for all of them. So they build all these marginal features that everyone needs. But this slows them down. Both in the initial creation and also in the subsequent support and maintenance. And it weakens their marketing message because they can't take a strong stand about who it's for and how it's going to make their life better.
The big wordcount reductions in my books were only possible after I had taken a strong stand on both who the book was really for (i.e. customer segment) and what it was promising to do for them (i.e. value proposition).
The Mom Test's original offering was:
"Everyone interested in business should read this book. If you don't know anything about startups, I'll explain what those are. And if you don't know about Lean Startup, I'll explain that too. And then once you know all that, I'm going to convince you that you should do customer interviews. And once you believe that, I'll tell you how to do it properly."
Wow. That's awful. How could I have thought that would be a compelling product?
Eventually, thanks to repeated complaints from beta readers, I changed it to this:
"If you're an entrepreneur who is trying to do customer interviews but having a bunch of trouble, then this book will help you understand what's going wrong and how do it properly."
Of course, nobody sees this value proposition. It lives in your head. But it influences every big decision you make about your book. And if you've never thought about it, then an automatic value proposition is put into your head, and it always tries to serve everyone. This is a bad thing and leads to long, bad books. As soon as is possible, you'll want to make it explicit, allowing (and forcing) yourself to make the hard, intentional choice about who it's for.
In terms of value proposition and customer definition, the second version is so much better. And as an author, the second version is a lifeline which lets me pull myself out of the mud of serving everyone. It allows me to imagine exactly who will be reading my book, as well as what they need and what they know. And then I can delete all the crap which isn't relevant for them.
That's how I ended up cutting 60% of each of my books in the final edits. Not by deleting words or paragraphs, but by excizing entire concepts which were either irrelevant or already known by the people I chose to serve. And that's why they love it. Because it's built for them and speaks precisely to their need, with zero unnecessary pages.
👨
As mentioned, in The Mom Test, I eventually decided that my readers would already know about startups, and would also already be convinced that customer interviews were a good idea. This allowed me to start with a bang, delivering real value about how to do it, from the very first page.
🤷
What if your book really is applicable to multiple audiences? I would reply that a focused book may be more transferrable than you suspect. For example, Sun Tzu's Art of War can apply to both military generals and corporate executives; would it be a better book if it was twice as long in order to explicitly speak to both? Hmm... Your readers aren't dumb; they're able to transfer lessons from one context to another. And if they can't, then it's your lucky day because you're sitting on a money machine. Just rebrand and reedit the book for each audience, as Michael Gerber did with the excellent The E-Myth Revisited (for small business owners). Following the book's well-deserved success, he multiplied his earnings by launching a whole family of mostly-identical books such as The E-Myth Chiropractor. (And many more.)
Deciding what to delete by narrowing the value proposition
⏳
Short version: it's okay for your book to solve a focused, important problem while ignoring all the problems and background around the problem.
If your book is solving a problem, then it's very easy to get pulled into answering an endless series of adjacent questions. For example: Why is this problem important? What are all the different ways to solve it? Why is it typically solved in this way rather than that? What are the exceptions? What's the historical context behind all that? How did the toolchain develop? What are those tools and how does one use them? How does one become a consultant or coach and help other people through all of this?
Oh man.
Now, while the core value of your book might be to attack any of the above, it is extremely unlikely that you will ever succeed in writing a book which covers all of them. And even if you did, nobody would want to read it since it's impossible to imagine a hypothetical reader who cares about all that stuff.
I fell into this trap with The Workshop Survival Guide. My (extremely bad) logic went like this:
"I'm teaching them how to run workshops. But some people also need to sell tickets, so I'll teach content marketing. And channel partnerships. So a bit of negotiation. Others will be working for clients, so I need to explain client management. And a little sales. And proposals. And I mention that professional slides are important, so I'll explain how to create a theme. And some fundamentals of typography. And what if they're getting overbooked and need to hire staff? And..."
It got a bit silly. Thankfully, I eventually realized my error and re-scoped it to:
"Assuming you have a workshop to run, this book will help you design and run the best one possible, with no drama or stress."
That's it. As an author, that was doable, whereas the initial version was an unreltenting Sisyphean torment. So I opened a beer and my manuscript, deleted 70k words (which I saved in another file for potential future use, obviously), and finally got the damn thing launched. Felt good.
👨
I pasted all the deleted bits into another file, of course, so none of it is wasted. I suspect that it will eventually morph into either content marketing, expanded editions, or even small standalone add-ons.
Front-load the reader's payoff and avoid withholding value
⏳
Short version: Structure your content such that a reader receives most of the value from the start of the book. This allows readers who abandon early to still receive value, and to therefore give referrals.
🎅
Possibly not real: For every other section in this guide, my advice is built on a credible foundation of data. This section, however, is pure belief and I have absolutely zero evidence. That being said, I believe it with sufficient conviction that I use this approach for all my stuff.
From the reader's perspective, your book has promised to deliver some clear, valuable takeaway.
With each additional hour they spend reading it, they're essentially trading an extra unit of time to extract the next portion of promised value.
Depending on how you organize the book, that experience can behave in one of these three ways:
1.
The valuable learning is distributed linearly throughout the book, and someone who abandons half-way through will still have gained half the value
2.
The value is withheld until the end, either because the author wants to wait "to give them the big secret" or because the payoff relies on significant, unavoidable theoretical groundwork
3.
The value is front-loaded, and someone who abandons half-way will still have gained almost all of the value.
A high-quality scientific diagram representing the three options. The red line shows the amount of value received by a reader who abandons early.
Here's why this is important:
A significant portion of your readers will stop reading before the end (or will recklessly skim). Their liklihood to recommend your book is based on the amount of value which was delivered to them before abandonment.
As such, if you withhold value—either intentionally or accidentally—then you are decimating your own word of mouth.
It's relatively rare for a non-fiction author to withhold value until the end. (Though it's one of the dominant shapes of fiction, where either loose ends are tied together or the reader is hit by a major twist.) It does sometimes get used by folks who are optimizing around 'time spent' instead of 'value received' (or getting ready to upsell into larger product). But as far as the book goes, I think it's a mistake. Deliver your value as quickly and completely as you are able, even if that means "giving away the secret" in the opening paragraph. If the book only works (or somehow becomes more effective) by withholding value, then fine. But doing so comes at a high cost, so be sure you have a reason.
Linear pay-off, on the other hand, is extremely common in non-fiction. And doubly so after the explosion of authors imitating the Gladwellian style of anecdotal non-fiction. In these sorts of books, each chapter is a largely standalone unit which culminates in some sort of lesson-learned or guiding principle. If a reader abandons early, they still get value from the bits they read. Most how-to books are structured like this, also; if you read half of them, then you know how to do half the stuff. It's not the worst! But you can do better.
I believe you maximize word of mouth by front-loading the value. Give it all away. Put it in the introduction. Hell, put it on the front cover, if you can manage. Start with a bang. Can you structure your book such that, if someone gives up half-way, they've received 90% of the value? Maybe not. It's hard. I'm not sure I'm able to do it. But I attempt to do it.
The Mom Test delivers probably 90% of its value in the first 50 pages (i.e. the first third). The other two thirds are really just supplemental detail for folks who need a bit more hand-holding. The Workshop Survival Guide was harder, because it's more of a collection of small tools (which all depend on each other) than it is a single major concept. So the graph there looks a bit more linear. I front-loaded as much as was possible (including several full rewrites), but it was only possible to get so far for the nature of that message.
This is paradoxical advice. On the one hand, you want to do everything in your power to make your book so easy and pleasant to read that people are devouring it in one sitting. On the other hand, you want to front-load the value such that if people give up, they've still gotten what they came for.
☑️
Do this now: Look at your table of contents. Where does the value arrive? How much would someone leave with if they abandoned half-way through? Or after ten minutes? Can you frontload it further to deliver more value (and thus unlock more recommendations) from the folks who abandon?

Word of mouth must be seeded within each community
Before we continue, pause to consider these two marketing mysteries:
I made guest appearances on probably 20 podcasts to talk about The Mom Test. I sold zero books across nineteen of those appearances. Whereas the twentieth immediately—and permanently—bumped my sales, increasing my book income by several thousand dollars per month in exchange for an hour's chit-chat.
The Mom Test has been fan-translated into ten languages. It's a #1 best seller in English and Russian. Whereas it sells roughly zero copies per month in the other eight languages. Why?
Here's how it works: your global audience is composed of a number of isolated "islands". An "island" could be a geography (like England) or a worldview (like "scalable startups"). Within a given island, everyone is talking to and learning from each other, so you can spread across the whole island through word of mouth after seeding an initial audience. However, there's not nearly as much transfer of advice between different islands.
The one breakthrough podcast was a big deal because it seeded my audience on a new island. I had already hit critical mass for the audience of "EU scalable startups". But I was entirely unknown by the audience of "profit-oriented bootstrappers". Since the product (i.e. the book) was already fairly honed, it was able to start quickly spreading through this newly accessed island. Hence the bump in earnings.
The same thing can happen geographically. Although only 10% of my potential audience is based in the UK, 80% of my customers for the first two years were from there. Why? Because that's where I seeded my initial audience, so that's where the word of mouth began and stayed. The book gradually migrated into western Europe, and then central/eastern, and then finally reached America after 3-4 years.
This also explains why most of my foreign-language editions sell so poorly: the non-English speakers of those languages are not engaging with the English-speaking recommenders of the book. Those new customers are unreachable by the word of mouth from my current customers.
This points toward a guiding strategy:
You must find some way to hustle together an original "seed audience" to begin the word of mouth within each community.
So, how does that work? Without getting dragged down into tactical marketing, I'll share a couple things that have worked for me. But if these seem non-doable for you, don't worry. You'll be able to do something else. Thanks to the strong word of mouth baked into your book's design, you won't need to do any of these marketing activities indefinitely, so it's okay if it feels inefficient and awkward.
I seeded Russia by launching at a large startup conference where they gave away 500 copies. (I also gave a terrible talk with the "help" of a live translator who apparently completely misunderstood the core metaphor of the book.) And although I never did anything else, that one fertile seed allowed the book to take root and spread throughout Russia, where it's now on its fourth print run.
Okay, well, what if you can't get conference invitations?
The bootstrapping community was seeded via the aforementioned podcast. But what if you don't know podcasters? Well, I didn't either. I received that invitation because I got an alert that someone had done a nice startup postmortem and mentioned the book. I wrote them an email to say hello and offer my support, and they invited me on the show. That sort of thing has happened a lot, and I try to keep an eye on both Google Alerts, Twitter, and few communities of potential readers for just that reason. I haven't done any cold outreach since the book first launched, but I certainly try to be responsive and helpful for the people who are talking about it already.
I'm a big believer in giving away books. If somebody who is in frequent contact with your potential readers is willing to accept a free book, then give it to them. Devin and I are currently going through this process with The Workshop Survival Guide, and have (so far) mailed out about 50 signed copies to various people. Giving away hundreds at a time at an event is even better, but only if you're absolutely certain that those attendees are in your target audience, and that it's done in a way which is going to make them take the book seriously.
All of these tasks require a lot of effort for a low number of initial sales/readers. But remember that you aren't trying to make money or reach scale with these tactics. (Unless you've got a lot of time on your hands.. You're just trying to seed the word of mouth in that particular community.
Of course, the book needs to be sufficiently "good" (in terms of the importance of the problem it solves and the quality of the solution it offers) for this to accomplish anything. Otherwise the book will just sit on a shelf, unread and unrecommended, and your sales will return to their baseline of zero. Obsessing over the creation of a brilliant product will allow you to get by with haphazard, temporary marketing. Trying to sell a faulty product will just get you into a never-ending, low-paying, unwanted new job.

Notes on creativity and the writing process
⏳
Short version: Create dedicated time with no opportunity for distraction and then force yourself to show up, sit down, and put the first few words on paper. Do this every day.
I've read (and enjoyed) a bunch of a books about the creative process. Here's what I've learned:
It's trendy to believe that writing relies on waiting for inspiration. But this leads to a trap where you don't do any work if you aren't feeling inspired. That's a problem, because while there are certainly inspiring and exciting moments throughout the process of writing a book, much of it is a pure slog.
As such, many of the tips offered by prolific writers are about how to keep making progress even when you aren't inspired.
Pick a sacred writing time. Make it shorter than you'd ideally like to write, but still substantial. (It's a required minimum time commitment, so you can just continue longer if things are going well.) So if you'd like to write for three hours each day, define one hour with a clear starting point and mark it in your calendar as inviolable. And here's the advice: during that hour, you don't have to write, but you aren't allowed to do anything else. No web browser, no phone alerts, no conversation. Just sit there and stare at the wall, with the blank screen or page in front of you. Once your hour (or whatever) is done, then you can get up and be done for the day, even if you didn't write a single word. The point of this strategy is to simmer in boredom until the unpleasant task of writing starts to seem, to your brain, like a luxury escape from the staggering boredom you've thrust upon it.
👨
I'm extremely prone to distraction at home, so I defend my writing time by going to cafes without internet (and leaving my phone at home). As an added perk, this has allowed me to write from beautiful spaces which folks can't normally work from. For example, I've written significant portions of my books from bodegas, museums, bookstores, public gardens, a sailboat, and various other peaceful spots willing to lend me a seat.
Find a way to begin. Much writing advice focuses on the importance of just getting the first word of the day written. Tim Ferriss required himself to write one page per day, even if it was garbage. And most days, he'd end up writing far more, and it wouldn't be garbage. We get blocked because we don't know how to make something great. But by just writing anything, even if you know it's crap, then you can get over the hump and start making something. And it can become great later, during editing and rewriting and refinement.
A related tip, from Julia Cameron's The Artist's Way, is to begin each day by writing for yourself. Having taught thousands of people to do it successfully, she believes that the absolute foundation of any creative practice is daily pages. Daily pages are stream of consciousness brain-dumping of whatever's on your mind, done first thing each day before starting your real work. You must fill at least one A4 page (which takes about ten minutes) and are free to continue on for as long as it's helpful. Also, you're not allowed to pause and think — you need to keep writing whatever's on your mind until the page is full. The practice gets the words flowing, loosens you up, and will often help bubble up ideas or worries or priorities that you didn't quite know you had.
👨
Although I was skeptical of how fluffy it sounded, I took Julia's advice and committed to try daily pages for one month. Life changing. Although I've currently lapsed, I kept it up every day for several years, and it quickly became the most important part of my day. Many of my pages weren't even coherent; they'd begin with something like: "Do do do beee dooo feeling... stressy? Tired? Hrm. Not well, overall. Not enough coffee? Chapter 2 is still a hot mess. No idea how to fix it. Feeling weird about that party last night... Couldn't relax into it." And by simply continuing to transcribe these brain noises, I would soon find myself thinking real thoughts and excited to get back to work.
Do the work. In The War of Art, Steven Pressfield adjusts the myth of the muse by saying that yes, she's out there, and yes, she's going to help you, but only if you're already moving on your own. The muse is a tailwind; she gets behind you and helps you make it to the finish line once you've already dragged yourself through half the race on your own. But she's never going to pick you up off the couch. You gotta get started doing the work, evey day, on your own. Even if you're feeling shitty and uninspired.
When you're navigating a labyrinth, remember that going backwards is part of moving forwards. While writing the early versions, you'll often hit a dead end and then need to backtrack and undo the previous days' or weeks' worth of work. This hurts. But it's a mistake to see it as losing progress. If you were in a maze, you might even see it as good news: you now know that branch is a dead end, and you've eliminated it from your search, which means you're that much closer to finding the right way. If you can keep the maze mindset, then you'll start to see those rewrites and deletions as the forward progress of eliminating dead ends. This mindset helps avoid becoming frustrated, which will surely undermine your ability to do the work.
When you're moving a pile of bricks, you just need to put in the hours. I don't like editing. I get zero creative joy from honing a sentence. So when I'm doing it, I need to frequently remind myself that it's a task like moving a pile of bricks: the more hours you spend on it, the more of the bricks you've moved. There are no shortcuts and no clever workarounds. You put in the time, and eventually the bricks end up where they're supposed to be. You don't need to wait for inspiration when the job is brick-moving. You just show up, grit your teeth, and start slogging.

The non-work of printing, selling, and distributing physical books on Amazon
Amazon has done a standout job of supporting self-publishers. The ebook tools are great, of course. That should come as no surprise.
But even for selling hard copies, you don't ever need to worry about manufacturing, inventory management, or warehousing.
You just upload two PDFs (contents and cover) and then, whenever someone buys a copy of the paperback, the book is literally printed, bound, and put in a box for shipping. It's a locally manufactured, on-demand, zero-inventory supply chain that you get absolutely for free.
Royalty subtleties
Amazon pays royalties with a two month delay, at the end of each month.
E.G. the earnings from your January sales will arrive at the end of March.
Kindle (ebook) royalties are the full 70% for books priced at less than $9.99/£9.99/€9.99. If you charge more than that, royalties drop to 35%. Consequently, you always want to price your kindle version at 9.99 or below.
Profit margins are slightly lower on print copies, since you end up getting your 70% cut after printing costs. On my books, printing costs are somewhere around $2.50-3.50 per copy. Charging $20 for the paperback, it meand I end up receiving ($20-$3)*0.7=$12, or about 60% of the cover price.
The cost of printing scales linearly with page count, but can be significantly more expensive for extras like full-color or glossy pages. In fact, running the numbers, I'm not sure it's currently possible to make a profit from a full-color book unless you're charging an unusually high cover price.
Page layout and creating files for print, kindle, and PDF
Before sending your book files to Amazon (or a print shop), you'll need to lay them out for print. There are currently five main tools, depending on the balance of fiddliness vs. control that you intend to subject yourself to.
I've used all of the first four options for various editions of my books. They all do the job, and they're all equally infuriating to use in their own special way. Pick your poison:
Vellum is the best and easiest (OSX only), although it has weak support for some stuff like tables and footnotes which may be a dealbreaker if you use lots of them. It costs about $250.
Scrivener is very powerful and you can do everything in it (including writing if you like). But it's extremely fiddly and doesn't follow the conventions of most writing/layout software. I eventually grew to like it, but you'll need to be willing to spend a few hours in tutorials and Googling around before it makes any sense at all. It costs about $50.
MS Word also works. You can download a template for print layout and then paste your chapters into it. Or you can do the settings yourself, although it's fairly fiddly and uses some rarely-used features and settings which you'll need to do some deep Googling for. (Mostly for stuff like having different headers on alternating pages and ensuring that new chapters always begin on a right-hand page.) You probably already own this, so it's essentially free.
Amazon has a small app called Kindle Creator which does what it says on the tin (Kindle only), although it's a bit fiddly with images and bullet lists. It's free.
For heavily designed layouts, consider Adobe InDesign. But be aware that many ebook readers (including older Kindles) will be unable to handle fancy layouts, so you're limiting your digital audience a bit.
This list will change and gain new entries, of course. But as of late 2019, this is pretty much your set of options.
Bulk orders for universities, corporates, conferences, and branded copies
I use a print shop to make books in bulk for large orders from universities/conferences/corporates.
This slightly reduces the per-copy cost, but is mostly worth doing because it's faster than having large numbers of books created all at once by Amazon print-on-demand. It also lets you give discounts to your bulk customers and to do custom-branded print runs, which they love.
I don't want to mess with logistics, so I looked around until finding a high-quality book printer which was able to offer warehousing and fulfilment. I typically keep about 200-500 copies printed and in their warehouse. If a client orders a couple hundred books, I just send a shipping request to my printers and the books automagically arrive a few days later. It's a good deal.
Allow a few days extra the first time you try to work with a print shop since they'll want slightly tweaked cover formatting and have lots of print options (page weight, cover lamination, etc.). They'll be happy to give advice and help you get your files in order, but it does take a little back-and-forth.
(I use Lonsdale Direct, based in the UK, for all my British and European bulk orders. They're great. If you end up using them, tell them I sent you so they give me free stuff.)
IP and liability
When I got business insurance, they wouldn't cover liabilities coming from my books unless I had a book lawyer review everything. I decided to take the risk and leave myself uninsured against book stuff, but it might be something to be aware of, in which case you would either move your books into a separate legal entity where they are firewalled, or spend the time to have them checked for accidental libel etc.
Translators obviously need to sign a bit of paper saying you've got all the rights.
If you use anyone's name or business name, you ideally get a note from them saying they're cool with that.
If your book becomes successful, people will pirate it. Some will simply find ways to steal/distribute (a victimless crime). But some will try to make some money by uploading their own version to Amazon (using either a copy of your ebook or scans of your paperback). In either case, you may want to pursue these folks a bit—even if it's fruitless—simply to create a digital paper trail which proves that you're attempting to enforce and maintain your intellectual property. Others will create bad summaries of your (already short) book which they'll sell for slightly cheaper. Although I find the summarizers to be vastly more irritating than the pirates, you probably just need to live with them.
Accounting and taxes (EU VAT)
For my European friends:
There's no VAT (0%) on physical books
There is VAT (20%) on digital books
However, the VAT on digital books is paid by the book selling platform (e.g. Amazon, Apple Books, udemy, Gumroad, etc.) and will be added to the purchase price of your EU customers automatically
Furthermore, you, the author, are not actually earning your money from selling digital books. You are technically earning your money through royalties, for which there is no VAT (0%)
Therefore, you don't need to account for or pay VAT on your book earnings

The goal: passive income and early retirement
⏳
Short version: As a product category, books are cheap to create, easy to distribute, require zero maintenance or customer support, and are something that people will gladly pay for. This makes them an extremely good source of passive income, if done properly.
Twelve years ago, I dropped out of grad school to start a startup, pursuing the default goal of trying to make it as big and valuable as possible. And we did manage to make a bit of progress in that direction. We ended up with some of the world's best investors, some big customers, some nice TV appearances, and ultimate failure. Afterwards, as I picked up the pieces and got ready to start my next business, I did a bit of thinking about why I was bothering to put myself through it all again.
And I realized that my main interest wasn't in building something huge or taking over the world. My goal in working was to stop needing to work. I didn't want to wait until 65 to retire; I wanted to get there by 30. To be clear, I didn't want to be rich. I just wanted enough passive income (i.e. money automatically hitting my bank account regardless of whether I was working or not) to never work again. And although it came a bit later than I had hoped, I did end up getting there two years later, at 32. And much to my surprise, it didn't happen through a business. It happened with a book.
Upon further reflection, it makes a certain sort of sense. Because books are actually incredible for achieving this particular goal. For example, everyone these days is fantasizing about being able to write software. Well, I can. I have a degree in computer science. And let me tell you: software is a pain. Even a simple app is vastly harder to create than a book, and it also requires both (1) other people and (2) ongoing maintenance. You can't work on an app for a year and then stop. Customer questions will go unanswered, the database will break, and a new phone or browser will launch that you need to go back and figure out how to support.
Plus, folks are far happier to pay for a book than a tech product. When's the last time you bought a $20 phone app? Tough sell. But a $20 book? Easy impulse buy. That makes a difference.
Or what about a physical product? I made a boardgame once, and let me tell you, that wasn't much fun. It was Kickstarted semi-successfully for about $90k in sales, $80k of which was immediately gobbled up by production costs. By the time we'd finished shipping orders all over the world and finally got paid, I had earned far below minimum wage.
I've spent the better part of a decade searching for a business or product which could be "set and forget". I wanted to make something good and then watch the money trickle in regardless of whether I was showing up to the office or not. I have found nothing in the tech world which is even close. It's possible, but it requires building a whole business (staff, processes, management, etc.) in addition to a product. There's a popular quote in the tech world that "passive income isn't"; everything in tech needs maintenance.
But books aren't easy either. Nearly a million books were self-published in 2018, and almost all of them earn nothing. Writing any sort of book is difficult and admirable, but it isn't enough. You need to write a certain type of book in a certain way.
So that's where I'm coming from with this guide. I don't have any intention of helping you overcome writer's block and finish your passion project; there are other books for that. Where I think I've got unique experience—and what I want to help you achieve—is in creating a book which you can ignore while free (but well-earned) money appears in your bank account. I used to have an SMS alert set up to ping me whenever someone bought a book. It felt good to hear the sales arrive and know that dinner had just been paid for. But it soon got to be too much, and I disabled the alerts. That felt good, too.
All that being said, you can't approach this task cynically. Writing a good book is a monstrous undertaking. It will kick you down the street and then laugh at you while you're huddled and crying. You aren't going to get through it unless you're writing something that matters to you, for people who need it, and for people you care about. There's no easy money here. But assuming you care about what you're writing, then I think it's well worth doing.
